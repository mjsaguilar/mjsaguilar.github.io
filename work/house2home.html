GV Design Sprint UI/UX Design E-Commerce Web Solo · 5 Days

House2Home:
Budget-Friendly Spaces

Designing an e-commerce platform that helps users find the perfect home decor starter kit — achieving a cohesive, aesthetic look without the overwhelm or the overspending.

Role
UI/UX Designer
Method
GV Design Sprint
Duration
5 Days
Tools
Figma, Photoshop
Overview

The challenge

House2Home is an e-commerce startup specializing in home interior decor and accessories. Moving into a new place should feel exciting — but for most people, the decorating process quickly becomes overwhelming. Customers told House2Home they felt paralyzed by choice and lacked confidence that the items they picked would actually look good together.

As the sole UI/UX designer, I ran a modified Google Ventures Design Sprint to ideate, prototype, and test a solution in just 5 days.

01
Overwhelm by options
Customers felt paralyzed sifting through endless individual items with no guidance on what would work together.
02
Lack of confidence
Fear of mismatched items led users to abandon the process entirely rather than risk wasting money on things that don't look right.
03
Budget anxiety
Achieving a cohesive aesthetic on a budget felt nearly impossible without knowing which combination of items to buy.
The Solution

Starter kits

Design an e-commerce platform that guides users to a custom home decor starter kit — a curated set of items that work together to achieve a specific aesthetic, all within budget. No more endless scrolling. No more second-guessing.

The platform would walk users through a short style quiz to understand their taste and space, then surface a personalized starter kit they could adjust, preview, and purchase with confidence.

Design Sprint Process

Five days, one solution

The GV Sprint framework forced fast, focused decision-making — no time to overthink, just build, test, and learn.

GV Design Sprint — 5-day process overview
Mon
Day 1 — Map

I started by reviewing existing user research — an interview, customer insights, and a persona — then used an affinity map to organize the themes into actionable design opportunities. Key pain points that emerged: difficulty finding cohesive decor, lack of purchasing confidence, feeling overwhelmed, and budget constraints.

Tue
Day 2 — Sketch

Lightning demos from Havenly, Pottery Barn, and IKEA surfaced how competitors approach style discovery and product browsing. Then it was straight into Crazy 8s — 8 ideas in 8 minutes — and solution sketches for the three key screens: landing, style quiz, and starter kit results.

Wed
Day 3 — Decide

Day 3 was about committing. I created a 15-panel storyboard mapping the full user journey — from landing to style quiz to starter kit reveal — and made a deliberate decision to cut several quiz screens that risked overwhelming users. Fewer questions, faster path to results.

15-panel storyboard

Thu
Day 4 — Prototype

Sketches became screens. The biggest challenge was honoring the original design direction under time pressure while fighting the urge to over-refine. The result was a realistic, testable prototype ready for Day 5.

Fri
Day 5 — Test

5 moderated usability tests — both remote and in-person — using the GV Sprint "5 Acts" framework. All participants successfully completed the given tasks and responded positively to the overall flow. The process felt simple and intuitive. Areas for improvement were noted for the next iteration.

Reflection

What I learned

My first solo design sprint was both intimidating and energizing. The time constraints forced a kind of creative decisiveness I hadn't fully experienced before — no room to overthink, just build and trust the process.

  • Speed unlocks creativity — tight deadlines pushed me to make bolder decisions faster and trust my instincts more than I would have with unlimited time.
  • Cutting is a design decision — deliberately removing quiz screens to reduce friction was one of the most impactful choices of the sprint.
  • Perfection is the enemy — learning to deliver "good enough to test" rather than "perfect before testing" was the biggest mindset shift of this project.
