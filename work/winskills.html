Overview

The challenge

Winskills is a training and consulting company that's been helping organizations build skills and develop talent for over 30 years. Despite their depth of expertise, their website told a different story — cluttered navigation, unclear messaging, and a visual language that didn't reflect the caliber of their work.

Over 4 weeks, our 3-person design team partnered directly with the CEO to diagnose the core problems and redesign the site from the ground up — with a focus on clarity, trust, and making it effortless for visitors to understand what Winskills offers and take action.

Problem Space

What was broken

Before touching a single frame in Figma, we needed to understand why the existing site wasn't working. We ran a heuristic evaluation and conducted user interviews to identify the biggest friction points.

01
Confusing Navigation
Users couldn't quickly identify the services offered. The menu structure was flat and didn't reflect the hierarchy of offerings.
02
Weak Visual Hierarchy
Pages lacked clear visual flow. Everything competed for attention — nothing felt like the obvious next step for a visitor.
03
Unclear Value Proposition
The homepage didn't clearly communicate what Winskills does or who it's for within the first few seconds of landing on the page.
Research

Listening first

We conducted a competitive analysis to benchmark against similar training and consulting companies, and ran a heuristic evaluation using Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics to systematically audit the existing site.

We then brought in real users for interviews to understand how they currently navigate professional development resources online and what signals build trust for them when evaluating a new vendor.

A few key patterns emerged from our research that shaped the direction of the redesign:

↗
Trust signals matter immediately
Users formed opinions about credibility within seconds. Social proof, clear credentials, and professional imagery were the biggest trust drivers.
↗
Simplicity over comprehensiveness
Users didn't want to read everything — they wanted a clear path to what was relevant to them. Less is more when the wayfinding is good.
Process

How we worked

With a 4-week timeline and a client who was deeply involved in the vision, we moved fast and stayed collaborative — checking in with the CEO at every major milestone to make sure our design decisions aligned with the business goals.

1
Discovery & Audit
Ran a full heuristic evaluation of the existing site. Documented usability violations and identified quick wins vs. structural problems that needed bigger solutions.
2
Competitive Analysis
Analyzed 5+ competing training and consulting sites to identify design patterns, content structures, and trust-building strategies that were working in the market.
3
User Interviews
Conducted interviews to understand how target users evaluate professional development vendors online — what builds trust, what causes doubt, and what makes them take action.
4
Information Architecture
Restructured the site's navigation and content hierarchy based on research insights. Simplified the menu and created a cleaner path from homepage to services to contact.
5
Wireframes → Hi-Fi Mockups
Moved from low-fidelity layouts to high-fidelity designs in Figma, iterating with CEO feedback at each stage to balance design vision with business requirements.
6
Prototype & Handoff
Built an interactive prototype in Figma to demonstrate the final flow, and prepared detailed design specs for development handoff.
Design Decisions

What we built

The redesign centered on three core principles: clarity first (every element earns its place), trust by design (professional signals that build confidence), and effortless navigation (get users where they need to go in as few clicks as possible).

Reflection

What I learned

This internship pushed me to work at the intersection of user needs and business constraints — with a real client, a tight deadline, and decisions that actually mattered. I learned how to advocate for design decisions with evidence rather than preference, and how to stay user-centered even when stakeholder opinions pull in different directions.

Working in a team of three also sharpened my collaboration skills — we had to divide work efficiently, give each other honest feedback, and make sure our individual pieces felt like one cohesive design system by the end.

If I could go back, I'd push for more usability testing on the hi-fi prototype before final handoff — getting one more round of user feedback would have given us greater confidence in the solutions we landed on.
