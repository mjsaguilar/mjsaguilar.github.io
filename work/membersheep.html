Membersheep:
Subscription Management

Designing a solution to help users easily manage all their subscriptions in one platform — and never get caught off guard by a forgotten free trial again.

Role
Lead UX/UI Designer
Platform
iOS Mobile
Timeline
5 Months
Tools
Figma, FigJam
Overview

The problem

In today's increasingly digital lifestyle, subscriptions have become a daily necessity — streaming, software, fitness, lifestyle. But this convenience has introduced a growing problem: subscription chaos and financial mismanagement.

People sign up for free trials and forget to cancel. Autopay quietly drains accounts. There's no single place to see what you're actually paying for each month. A 2021 Chase Bank survey found that nearly 70% of consumers underestimate how much they spend on subscriptions monthly — often by $100 or more. Membersheep was designed to fix all of that.

01

Users have subscriptions scattered across multiple apps, banks, and email confirmations with no central place to track them all.

Without timely reminders, users frequently get charged after trial periods end — a small but frustrating and avoidable expense.

Financial Blind Spots
Autopay makes it easy to lose sight of where money is going. Users often don't realize how much they're spending on subscriptions monthly.
Solution

One place for everything

Membersheep is a mobile app that lets users manage all their subscriptions in one platform — by connecting a bank account or card, or entering details manually. Users can track free trials, get notified before they're charged, and cancel subscriptions without the usual friction.

Feature 01
Home Dashboard

A curated summary that instantly shows users the full picture of their subscription activity — total spend, active subscriptions, and upcoming charges all at a glance. Megan's frustration with not knowing her monthly total directly shaped this screen: the spending summary is the first thing she sees when she opens the app.

Feature 02
Add New Subscription

A simple step-by-step process that guides users through selecting and inputting their subscription details seamlessly — no friction, no confusion. Because 78% of survey respondents said they'd only use a tracker if setup took under two minutes, I kept this flow to four taps maximum.

Feature 03
Track Free Trials

The trial tracker lets users monitor remaining trial periods and get notified before they convert to paid — so they can make informed decisions without being caught off guard. This feature was the single most-requested outcome from user interviews, where every participant described getting charged by surprise at least once.

Discover

Research & insights

I conducted 5 user interviews and an online survey with 55 respondents who actively manage multiple subscriptions — one of the most substantial research efforts I'd undertaken at that point in my training. The goal was to understand real pain points, not assumptions, and let the data drive every design decision that followed.

Three themes emerged so consistently across both methods that they became the direct brief for the design: people needed a central hub, proactive alerts before charges hit, and a fast way to see their total monthly spend at a glance.

Primary research — survey results (55 respondents)

"I would like to get notified at least 7 days before I get billed for a subscription."

— User interview participant

This quote became a design constraint, not just a data point. It directly informed the 7-day default reminder built into the trial tracker — a specific, research-backed decision rather than an arbitrary number.

Define

Meet Megan

Interview patterns converged on a clear user archetype: a busy young professional who knows she's overpaying for subscriptions but doesn't have the time or system to do anything about it. I built Megan to give that archetype a name, a face, and a set of frustrations I could design against.

Megan's three core needs — visibility into spending, proactive alerts, and frictionless setup — became the non-negotiable requirements that every screen had to serve. When I faced a design decision, I asked: would this save Megan time, or add to her cognitive load?

"I'm forgetful and I think I'm just really busy and don't have time to manage all my subscriptions."

Deliver

Testing & iteration

I asked 5 people to navigate the wireframe prototype and observed where they hesitated, got confused, or had unmet expectations. Rather than asking "did you like it?", I gave each participant specific tasks to complete — watching where they stumbled told me far more than any preference question could. Two key findings directly shaped the final design.

Low-fidelity wireframes — tested with 5 participants
Finding 01

Add Subscription flow

The "Add a Custom Subscription" button placement confused testers — 4 out of 5 paused and re-read the screen before proceeding. The competing CTAs created decision paralysis at a critical step in the flow. It was removed and replaced with a single, clear "Continue" button, reducing the cognitive load and keeping the primary path unambiguous.

Finding 02

Active Subscriptions card

All 5 testers tapped "7 active subscriptions" expecting it to be a link — when nothing happened, they assumed the app was broken. This was a clear affordance failure. The card was redesigned to clearly surface three distinct actions: view active subscriptions, view trials, or add new — turning a passive stat into an actionable hub.

Reflection

What I learned

Membersheep was my first end-to-end UX project, and looking back, it's the one that taught me what design actually is. Not the visual layer — the thinking underneath it. Every screen on this project exists because a real person told me something frustrating about their life, and I tried to make it a little less frustrating.

  • Usability testing changes everything — both before/after iterations came directly from watching real people use the prototype. The "broken link" moment in Finding 02 would never have surfaced in a design review. Only real users revealed it.
  • Accessibility needs to be designed in, not retrofitted — I'd run a contrast audit and test with larger text sizes from the very first wireframe. Accessibility decisions made at hi-fi are ten times harder to fix than ones made at lo-fi.
  • Research is only useful if it informs decisions — the biggest shift in my thinking was learning to connect a data point to a specific design choice. "55 people said X" only matters if you can point to the screen it shaped.
  • Future scope — advanced spending analytics, personalized recommendations based on usage patterns, and a one-tap cancellation flow integrated directly with subscription providers would be the natural next features to explore.
